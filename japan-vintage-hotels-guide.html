Spring (March-May)

  • Cons: Cold weather, shorter daylight hours

Arrival & Check-in

  • Remove shoes at entrance (indoor slippers provided)
  • Welcomed with oshibori (hot towel) and tea
  • Room tour including how to use facilities
  • Yukata robe fitting (wear throughout stay)

  • Kaiseki dinner: Multi-course seasonal menu served in-room or private dining room (typically 6-7pm)
  • Breakfast: Traditional Japanese with grilled fish, miso soup, rice (7-8am)
  • Dietary restrictions accommodated with advance notice

Onsen Etiquette

  1. Wash thoroughly at shower stations before entering bath
  2. No swimsuits â€” baths are gender-separated or private
  3. Small towel for modesty (don't submerge in water)
  4. Tattoos may be restricted (private baths solve this)

Do I need to speak Japanese?

Top-tier ryokans in Kyoto, Hakone, and Tokyo have English-speaking staff. Rural properties may have limited English â€” translation apps help. VintageTrips provides concierge assistance and pre-arrival briefings.

Are children allowed?

Most ryokans accept children, but luxury properties often set minimum ages (typically 12+). Check policies before booking. Some specialize in family experiences with kid-friendly meals.

What should I pack?

Ryokans provide yukata robes, toiletries, and slippers. Bring: casual clothing for daytime, comfortable walking shoes, camera, any specific toiletries you prefer. No need for formal wear â€” yukata is dinner attire.

Can I get a Western-style bed?

Some ryokans now offer hybrid rooms with beds + tatami areas. Vintage Western hotels (like Fujiya or Nikko Kanaya Hotel) have traditional beds. Specify preferences when booking.
