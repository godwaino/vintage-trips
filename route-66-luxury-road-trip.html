üóìÔ∏è Best Time to Travel Route 66

Optimal Seasons

  • Spring (April-May): Mild weather, wildflowers blooming, fewer crowds. Ideal for convertible driving.
  • Fall (September-October): Comfortable temperatures, autumn colors in the Midwest, perfect road trip conditions.
  • Late Spring/Early Fall: Balance of good weather and manageable tourist numbers.

Avoid If Possible

  • Summer (July-August): Extreme heat in desert sections (Arizona, New Mexico), crowded attractions.
  • Winter (December-February): Snow and ice in northern states, some attractions closed.

üöô The Vintage Route: State-by-State

Illinois: The Beginning (Chicago)

Must-See: Start at the iconic Route 66 "Begin" sign on Adams Street. Visit Lou Mitchell's diner (breakfast institution since 1923), explore Pontiac's Route 66 Hall of Fame & Museum.

Vintage Stay: The Pontiac Oakland Automobile Museum offers boutique lodging in a restored 1930s building.

Missouri: The Heart of Route 66

Must-See: St. Louis Gateway Arch, Devil's Elbow bridge, Springfield's Route 66 Car Museum, Meramec Caverns.

Vintage Stay: The Munger Moss Motel in Lebanon ‚Äî 1940s neon signs, period furnishings, friendly owners with decades of Route 66 stories.

Kansas: Short but Sweet (13 miles!)

Must-See: Galena's vintage gas stations, Cars on the Route (inspiration for Pixar's "Cars"), Old Riverton Store (1925).

Oklahoma: Red Earth & Roadside Giants

Must-See: Blue Whale of Catoosa, Pops 66 soda ranch, Oklahoma City's Route 66 Museum, Arcadia Round Barn.

Vintage Diner: Rock Cafe in Stroud ‚Äî built from local stone in 1939, authentic 1950s atmosphere.

Texas: Panhandle Scenery

Must-See: Cadillac Ranch (spray-paint welcome!), Big Texan Steak Ranch (72-oz steak challenge), Devil's Rope Museum (barbed wire history).

Vintage Stay: The Nat in Amarillo ‚Äî restored 1920s hotel with Art Deco details.

New Mexico: Desert Magic

Must-See: Tucumcari's Neon Row, Blue Swallow Motel (1939), Santa Fe Plaza, Albuquerque Old Town, Continental Divide marker.

Vintage Stay: El Rancho Hotel in Gallup ‚Äî 1930s Hollywood glamour, hosted John Wayne, Katharine Hepburn, Ronald Reagan.

Arizona: Desert Dreams

Must-See: Petrified Forest National Park, Meteor Crater, Wigwam Village Motel, Hackberry General Store, Seligman (birthplace of Route 66 preservation).

Vintage Diner: Delgadillo's Snow Cap in Seligman ‚Äî quirky 1950s charm, legendary humor, classic burgers.

California: The Final Stretch

Must-See: Roy's Motel & Cafe in Amboy, Bagdad Cafe, Calico Ghost Town, Santa Monica Pier (end of Route 66 sign).

Luxury Stay: Shutters on the Beach or Hotel Casa del Mar in Santa Monica for your grand finale.

‚ùì Frequently Asked Questions

Can I drive Route 66 in a rental car?

Yes! Most of Route 66 is paved and accessible in any vehicle. For the vintage experience, consider renting a classic car from companies like Classic Car Rentals LA or Chicago Classic Cars.

Is Route 66 still drivable?

About 85% of the original route is still drivable. Some sections have been replaced by Interstate highways, but well-marked "Historic Route 66" signs guide you along the authentic path.

Do I need a special license or insurance?

A standard driver's license is sufficient. Check with your car rental company about insurance coverage, especially for classic vehicles.

What's the difference between Route 66 and I-40?

Interstate 40 replaced much of Route 66 in the 1970s-80s. I-40 is faster but bypasses the small towns, vintage motels, and roadside attractions that make Route 66 special. The historic route parallels I-40 through most states.
